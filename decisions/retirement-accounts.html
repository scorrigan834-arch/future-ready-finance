Retirement Accounts Explained: 401(k), IRA, Roth & More

The alphabet soup of retirement accounts, decoded — plus a simple order for which to use first.

Guides › Retirement Accounts

401(k), IRA, Roth, Traditional — retirement accounts come with an alphabet soup of names that makes them feel more complicated than they are. Here's what each one actually does, and a simple order for which to use first.

Why these accounts exist

Retirement accounts are special because of tax advantages. The government wants you to save for retirement, so it lets these accounts grow without the usual taxes on investment gains. That tax break, compounded over decades, can mean tens of thousands of extra dollars versus a regular account.

The main accounts, in plain English

🏢 401(k)

Offered through your employer. Money comes straight from your paycheck, and many employers match part of what you contribute — free money. Higher contribution limits than an IRA.

🏦 IRA

An Individual Retirement Account you open yourself, separate from any job. More investment choices, but lower contribution limits than a 401(k).

Roth vs Traditional: the key fork

When You Pay the Tax

Both 401(k)s and IRAs come in two flavors. Traditional: you skip taxes now and pay them when you withdraw in retirement. Roth: you pay taxes now and withdraw completely tax-free later. The rule of thumb: if you expect a higher tax rate in retirement than today, Roth often wins. Lower? Traditional. Unsure? Many people split the difference.

Always grab the employer match first. If your job matches 401(k) contributions, contributing enough to get the full match is the highest-return move in personal finance — an instant 50–100% return before the market does anything. Never leave it on the table.

A simple order of operations

  • 1. 401(k) up to the match — capture every dollar of free employer money.
  • 2. Pay off high-interest debt — no account beats 20%+ credit card interest.
  • 3. Max an IRA (Roth if you qualify) — more investment freedom.
  • 4. Back to the 401(k) — contribute more, up to the annual limit.

What to actually invest in

Opening the account is step one — you still have to invest the money inside it (a common beginner mistake is leaving it in cash). For most people, a low-cost broad index fund or a target-date fund matched to your retirement year is a simple, sensible default. Set it, automate contributions, and let decades of compounding do the work.
