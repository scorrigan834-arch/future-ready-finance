Social Security Basics: What You Need to Know

Social Security will likely be part of your retirement, and the choices around it can be worth tens of thousands of dollars. Here is how it works and how to plan around it.

Home › Guides › Social Security Basics

Why this matters even if retirement feels far away: Social Security will likely be part of your retirement income, and decisions you make decades from now — like when to claim — can change your monthly benefit by tens of thousands of dollars over a lifetime. Understanding the basics early helps you plan around it.

What Social Security Actually Is

Social Security is a federal program that provides income in retirement, as well as disability and survivor benefits. You pay into it through the FICA tax on every paycheck — 6.2% from you, matched by 6.2% from your employer. In return, you earn credits toward future benefits.

How You Qualify

  • You earn "credits" by working and paying Social Security taxes — up to 4 credits per year
  • You generally need 40 credits (about 10 years of work) to qualify for retirement benefits
  • Your benefit is based on your highest 35 years of earnings — years with no earnings count as zeros, which can lower your average

How Your Benefit Is Calculated

The Social Security Administration averages your highest 35 years of inflation-adjusted earnings to determine your benefit. Higher lifetime earnings generally mean a higher benefit — but the formula is progressive, replacing a larger share of income for lower earners.

Full Retirement Age (FRA)

Your "full retirement age" is when you're entitled to 100% of your calculated benefit. For most people working today, FRA is 67. You can claim earlier or later — and that timing dramatically changes your monthly check.

The Single Most Important Decision: When to Claim

You can start benefits as early as 62 or as late as 70. When you claim has a permanent effect on your monthly amount.

Claiming AgeEffect on Monthly Benefit
62 (earliest)Permanently reduced — roughly 30% less than at FRA
67 (full retirement age)100% of your calculated benefit
70 (latest worth waiting for)Permanently increased — roughly 24% more than at FRA
The trade-off: Claiming early means smaller checks for more years; waiting means larger checks for fewer years. Delaying from 62 to 70 can increase your monthly benefit by more than 75%. The right choice depends on your health, other savings, and whether you need the income — there's no universal answer.

Will Social Security Be There for You?

This is a common and fair worry for younger workers. Here's a measured view: Social Security faces a long-term funding shortfall, and its trust funds are projected to be depleted in the coming decade or so. But that does not mean it disappears.

  • Even if the trust fund is depleted, ongoing payroll taxes are projected to still cover a large majority of scheduled benefits
  • Changes are likely — possible adjustments include a higher retirement age, higher payroll taxes, or modified benefit formulas, especially for higher earners
  • The prudent approach: plan as though Social Security will be one piece of your retirement, not the whole thing. Build your own savings (401k, IRA, HSA) so you're not dependent on it

The Smart Way to Think About It

Treat Social Security as a foundation that reduces how much you need to save on your own — not as your entire retirement plan. The more you build your own investments now, the more flexibility and security you'll have regardless of how the program evolves.

Other Benefits Worth Knowing

  • Disability benefits: If you can't work due to a qualifying disability, Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) may provide income
  • Survivor benefits: Your spouse and dependent children may receive benefits if you die
  • Spousal benefits: A lower-earning spouse may be able to claim based on their partner's record
The bottom line: Social Security is a valuable foundation, but it was never designed to fully fund retirement. Understand how it works, plan to claim it strategically, and build your own savings alongside it so your future doesn't depend on any single source.
