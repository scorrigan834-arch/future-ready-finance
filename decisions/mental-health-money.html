Mental Health and Money

Money and mental health are deeply connected. Financial stress affects your wellbeing, and your wellbeing affects your financial decisions. Understanding the link is the first step to managing both.

Home › Guides › Mental Health and Money

You are not alone: Financial stress is one of the most common sources of anxiety. Acknowledging the emotional side of money isn't weakness — it's the foundation of making better decisions.

How Money Affects Mental Health

Financial difficulty doesn't just affect your bank account. It can disrupt sleep, strain relationships, reduce focus, and contribute to anxiety and depression. The stress of debt, instability, or uncertainty about the future is real and measurable.

  • Chronic stress: Ongoing money worries keep your body in a heightened stress state, affecting physical and mental health
  • Shame and isolation: Many people feel embarrassed about their finances and avoid talking about them, which deepens the stress
  • Decision fatigue: Constant financial pressure makes every choice feel heavier, leading to avoidance or impulsive decisions

How Mental Health Affects Money Decisions

The relationship runs both ways. Depression, anxiety, and other conditions can directly shape financial behavior.

Depression and Avoidance

Depression can make opening bills, checking balances, or dealing with money feel overwhelming — leading to avoidance that makes problems worse over time.

Anxiety and Over-Control

Anxiety can lead to obsessive checking, extreme frugality even when unnecessary, or paralysis when facing financial decisions.

Emotional Spending

Shopping can become a way to cope with difficult emotions — providing temporary relief but often adding financial stress afterward.

Mania and Impulsivity

For those with bipolar disorder, manic episodes can involve impulsive spending or risky financial decisions that have lasting consequences.

Breaking the Cycle

Small, manageable steps can interrupt the stress-spending-stress loop. The goal isn't perfection — it's building gentle systems that reduce the emotional load.

  • Automate what you can: Automatic bill payments and savings transfers remove the emotional burden of remembering and deciding each time
  • Set a "worry window": Schedule a specific 30-minute time each week to deal with finances, so money stress doesn't bleed into every moment
  • Start impossibly small: If checking your balance feels overwhelming, start by checking just one account once a week. Build from there.
  • Create a 24-hour rule: For non-essential purchases over a set amount, wait 24 hours. This interrupts emotional spending without restriction guilt.
  • Talk to someone: Whether a trusted friend, partner, or professional, naming financial stress out loud reduces its power

The Connection Goes Both Ways

Improving your financial situation can improve your mental health — and improving your mental health can improve your financial decisions. You don't have to fix everything at once. Progress in either area helps the other.

When to Seek Help

If financial stress is affecting your sleep, relationships, work, or overall wellbeing, support is available and seeking it is a sign of strength.

  • Mental health support: A therapist or counselor can help with the emotional side of money. Many offer sliding-scale fees based on income.
  • Financial counseling: Nonprofit credit counseling agencies offer free or low-cost help with debt and budgeting
  • Crisis support: If you're struggling, the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988) provides free, confidential support 24/7
A gentle reminder: Your worth is not your net worth. Financial challenges are circumstances you're moving through, not a reflection of who you are. Be as patient with yourself as you would be with a friend.
