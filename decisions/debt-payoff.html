How to Pay Off Debt: Avalanche, Snowball, and What Actually Works

Debt is not a life sentence. There are proven strategies to pay it off faster — some mathematically optimal, some psychologically effective. Here's how to choose the right one for you.

Home › Guides › Debt Payoff

Before you strategize: List every debt you have — balance, interest rate, and minimum payment. You cannot build a payoff plan without this information. Most people underestimate both how much they owe and how much interest is costing them each month.

How Minimum Payments Work Against You

Credit card companies set minimum payments as low as possible — typically 1–2% of the balance or 5, whichever is more. This is intentional. Minimum payments are designed to maximize the interest you pay over the longest possible time.

The Two Main Payoff Strategies

Avalanche Method (Mathematically Optimal)

Pay minimums on all debts. Put every extra dollar toward the debt with the highest interest rate first.

  • Saves the most money in total interest
  • Best for people motivated by numbers and efficiency
  • The mathematically correct answer
  • Slower early wins — high-rate debt may have a large balance

Snowball Method (Psychologically Effective)

Pay minimums on all debts. Put every extra dollar toward the debt with the smallest balance first.

  • Creates quick wins that build motivation
  • Research shows better completion rates for many people
  • Costs slightly more in total interest
  • Best for people who need momentum to stay on track
Which to choose: If you're disciplined and motivated by math, avalanche saves more money. If you've tried and failed at debt payoff before, snowball may keep you engaged long enough to succeed. The best strategy is the one you'll actually stick to.

Step-by-Step: Building Your Payoff Plan

  1. List all debts — creditor, balance, interest rate, minimum payment. Use a spreadsheet or an app like Undebt.it (free).
  2. Find your extra payment amount — review your budget and identify how much above minimums you can commit each month. Even 0 extra accelerates payoff significantly.
  3. Choose your method — avalanche (highest rate first) or snowball (lowest balance first)
  4. Attack your target debt — pay minimums on everything, put every extra dollar at the target debt
  5. Roll payments when a debt is paid off — when one debt is gone, add its minimum payment to what you're paying on the next target. This is the "roll" in the snowball — your payment grows over time.
  6. Don't add new debt — if you're paying off credit cards, take them out of your wallet. Paying off 00 and charging 00 is a treadmill, not progress.

High-Interest vs Low-Interest Debt: Does It Make Sense to Invest Instead?

This is one of the most common questions in personal finance, and the math is actually clear:

Debt Interest RateRecommendation
Above 8–10%Pay down aggressively before investing. You cannot reliably earn more than 10% investing — guaranteed debt reduction beats uncertain market returns.
6–8%Gray zone. Capture your 401(k) match first (instant 50–100% return), then split remaining funds between debt and investing.
Below 5–6%Consider investing instead. Long-term market returns historically exceed low-rate debt costs — especially if it's student loan debt in the 3–5% range.
Any rate with employer 401(k) matchAlways capture the full match first. It's an instant guaranteed return that beats any debt payoff math.

Debt Consolidation and Refinancing

Sometimes the first step is reducing your interest rate before tackling the balance:

  • Balance transfer cards — many credit cards offer 0% intro APR for 12–21 months on transferred balances. Transfer high-rate debt and pay aggressively during the 0% window. Watch for balance transfer fees (typically 3–5%) and what rate kicks in after the intro period.
  • Personal loan consolidation — take a personal loan at a lower rate to pay off multiple high-rate debts. Simplifies payments and reduces interest cost. Only works if you qualify for a meaningfully lower rate and have the discipline not to run the cards back up.
  • Student loan refinancing — private refinancing can lower your rate, but you lose federal protections (income-driven repayment, forgiveness programs). Don't refinance federal loans without understanding what you're giving up.
