Can I Afford This Apartment?

Evaluate the full cost of living independently — not just the number on the listing.

Home › Guides › Apartment Affordability

The 30% Rule: Housing costs (rent + utilities) should ideally stay under 30% of your gross monthly income. In high-cost cities, aim for under 35% — but understand the tradeoffs.

Why Rent Is Just the Starting Point

The number on the listing is only part of the real cost. Most people underestimate their actual housing cost by $300–600/month.

The True Cost of an Apartment

Cost ItemTypical Monthly Range
RentListed amount
Utilities (electric, gas, water)$80–200+
Internet$50–80
Renters insurance$15–30
Parking$0–250+
Total above rent$145–560+

Example: $1,400/month apartment

Add utilities ($140) + internet ($60) + renters insurance ($20) + parking ($80) = $1,700/month actual cost. To stay under 30%, you need gross income of ~$5,667/month (~$68,000/year). At $48,000/year income, this apartment is 42.5% of your income — likely too high.

The Quick Affordability Check

  • Monthly gross income ÷ 3 = your maximum rent budget
  • Add all extra costs: utilities, internet, parking, insurance
  • After housing and other fixed expenses, do you still save at least 10%?

Questions to Ask Before Signing

  • What are the average utility costs for this unit? (Ask landlord for past 12 months)
  • Is parking included or extra?
  • What are the early termination penalties?
  • What happens to your budget if you have an unexpected $500 expense?
Move-in costs to budget for: Most apartments require first month + last month + security deposit upfront — often $2,800–4,200 on a $1,400/month apartment before you get the keys.

When It Makes Sense to Stretch

Sometimes a more expensive apartment is worth it if it significantly reduces commute cost or time, is in a location that advances your career, or includes utilities, parking, or gym (reducing other expenses). Make the decision consciously — not just reacting to a nice listing.
