Banking Basics: Everything You Need Before Anything Else

Before investing, budgeting, or building credit — you need a bank account set up correctly. Here's everything to know about where your money lives.

Home › Guides › Banking Basics

Start here if: You just got your first job, are opening your first account, or have a basic account but aren't sure you're using it right. This is the foundation everything else is built on.

Checking vs Savings: What Each One Is Actually For

Most people know they need both — but most people use them wrong. Here's the actual purpose of each account:

Checking Account

  • Your everyday spending account
  • Receives your paycheck (direct deposit)
  • Pays bills, rent, subscriptions
  • Linked to your debit card
  • Should never have more than 1–2 months of expenses in it
  • Typically earns 0% interest — money here should be working, not sitting

Savings Account

  • Your money storage account — not for daily spending
  • Emergency fund lives here
  • Short-term goals (car, travel, move)
  • Should earn interest — see HYSA below
  • Slightly harder to access by design (keeps you from spending it)
  • Keep 3–6 months of expenses here once you're employed

High-Yield Savings Accounts (HYSA): The Upgrade Most People Don't Know About

A traditional savings account at a big bank (Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo) typically pays 0.01% interest per year. That means 0,000 earns annually.

A high-yield savings account at an online bank currently pays 4–5% per year. That same 0,000 earns 00–500 annually — for doing absolutely nothing different.

Banks vs Credit Unions: What's the Difference?

Banks are for-profit companies owned by shareholders. They make money from fees and interest. Large national banks offer convenience — more ATMs, more branches, better apps.

Credit unions are nonprofit cooperatives owned by their members (you). Because they're not profit-driven, they typically offer lower loan rates, higher savings rates, fewer fees, and better customer service. The downside: fewer locations and sometimes older technology.

  • Most credit unions require you to qualify by living in a certain area, working for a certain employer, or belonging to a certain group — but many community credit unions are open to anyone in the area
  • Credit unions are insured by NCUA instead of FDIC — equally safe, up to 50,000
  • For a first account: either works. For loans later in life (car, mortgage), always check the credit union rate first

Overdrafts: The Fee That Catches Everyone Once

An overdraft happens when you spend more money than is in your account. Banks typically charge 5–35 per transaction when this happens. If you buy three things on a day your account is empty, that's 5–105 in fees — for purchases that might total 0.

  • Opt out of overdraft "protection" — it's not protection, it's a fee trap. When opted out, your card is simply declined if you have no money, which is the better outcome
  • Set up low-balance alerts — most banking apps let you get a text or notification when your balance drops below 0 or 00
  • Link a savings account as backup — if you do overdraft, many banks will pull from linked savings for free instead of charging a fee
  • Know your real balance — pending transactions may not show immediately. Your "available balance" is what you can actually spend

Direct Deposit: Set This Up Before Anything Else

Direct deposit means your employer sends your paycheck electronically straight to your bank account — no waiting for a check, no trip to the bank, money available the same day it's sent. Here's how to set it up:

  1. Log into your bank account and find your routing number (9 digits, identifies your bank) and account number (identifies your specific account)
  2. Give both numbers to your employer's HR or payroll department — usually a form called "Direct Deposit Authorization"
  3. Takes 1–2 pay cycles to activate. Until then you'll receive a paper check
Pro tip: Many banks unlock features, waive fees, or give you earlier access to paychecks when you set up direct deposit. It's almost always worth doing immediately.

Automating Your Money: The Most Powerful Habit

The goal is to make good financial behavior require zero willpower. Set these up once and they run forever:

  • Automatic savings transfer: The day your paycheck hits, automatically move a set amount to savings. Start with 5 if that's all you can manage — the habit matters more than the amount
  • Automatic bill payments: Any bill with a fixed amount (phone, internet, subscriptions) should be on autopay — eliminates late fees and stress
  • Variable bills with alerts: Bills that change monthly (utilities, credit cards) should have email alerts instead of autopay — so you see the number before it's charged

What You Need to Open an Account

  • Government-issued photo ID (driver's license, state ID, or passport)
  • Social Security number
  • A small opening deposit (some accounts require 5–100; many online banks require /bin/sh)
  • If under 18: a parent or guardian will need to be a joint account holder at most banks
