Your First Paycheck: A Complete Walkthrough

Your first real paycheck will look nothing like your salary. Here's every line explained — so nothing surprises you.

Home › Guides › First Paycheck

Before your first paycheck arrives: Make sure you've set up direct deposit with your employer, completed your W-4 (withholding form), and enrolled in any benefits (health insurance, 401k) during your onboarding window — these elections affect every paycheck.

A pay stub is the document that breaks down your paycheck — what you earned, what was taken out, and what you actually received. Here's what each section means:

Pay Periods: How Often You'll Be Paid

Pay SchedulePaychecks Per YearExample
Weekly52Every Friday
Biweekly26Every other Friday (most common)
Semi-monthly241st and 15th of each month
Monthly12Last business day of month

Biweekly vs semi-monthly matters for budgeting: Biweekly means two months per year you receive 3 paychecks instead of 2. Plan for this — the "extra" paycheck is a great opportunity to boost savings or pay down debt.

The Five Things to Do With Your First Paycheck

  1. Contribute enough to get your full 401(k) match — if you haven't enrolled yet, do it immediately. Every paycheck without the match is free money you're leaving on the table.
  2. Set up automatic savings — transfer a fixed amount to savings the day your paycheck hits. Even 0 builds the habit.
  3. Review your health insurance election — make sure you enrolled in a plan. Missing the open enrollment window means waiting until next year.
  4. Verify your W-4 withholding — if your paycheck looks significantly higher or lower than expected, check your W-4 settings.
  5. Check your beneficiaries — update 401(k) and life insurance beneficiary designations on day one. Many people forget this and have outdated names listed for years.
