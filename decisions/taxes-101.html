Taxes 101: How They Work and What You Actually Owe

Taxes are not optional and not as complicated as people make them sound. Here's everything you need to understand before your first paycheck or first tax return.

Home › Guides › Taxes 101

The most common tax myth: "If I get a raise and move into a higher tax bracket, I'll take home less money." This is completely false. Tax brackets only apply to income within that range — not all of your income. More money always means more take-home pay.

How Tax Brackets Actually Work

The US uses a progressive tax system, meaning different portions of your income are taxed at different rates. You don't pay the top rate on all your income — only on the portion above each threshold.

The W-4: Your Withholding Form

When you start a job, you fill out a W-4. This tells your employer how much federal tax to withhold from each paycheck. Getting this right means you won't owe a large amount at tax time — or overpay all year and wait for a refund.

  • The 2020+ W-4 is straightforward — follow the instructions, enter your other income sources if any, and it calculates the right withholding automatically
  • One job, no dependents, standard situation: Fill out Step 1 (personal info) and Step 5 (signature). Leave everything else blank. Done.
  • Multiple jobs or a side income: Use Step 2 to account for additional income so you're not undertaxed

W-2 vs 1099: Employee vs Contractor

This distinction determines how you pay taxes and how much of the work you're responsible for:

W-2 Employee

  • Employer withholds taxes from each paycheck
  • Employer pays half of Social Security and Medicare (6.2% + 1.45%)
  • Receive a W-2 form in January showing what you earned and what was withheld
  • File taxes once a year — usually straightforward
  • Eligible for unemployment insurance if let go

1099 Contractor / Freelancer

  • No taxes withheld — you receive full payment and pay taxes yourself
  • Pay self-employment tax (15.3%) on top of income tax — you pay both halves of Social Security and Medicare
  • Must pay quarterly estimated taxes (April, June, September, January)
  • Can deduct business expenses to reduce taxable income
  • Not eligible for unemployment insurance
If you have 1099 income: Set aside 25–30% of every payment you receive into a separate savings account for taxes. This is not optional. If you spend it and then owe it, the IRS charges penalties and interest on top of what you owe.

Filing Taxes for the First Time

The tax deadline is April 15 each year. You're filing a return for the previous calendar year (so in April 2025 you file for 2024).

  1. Gather your documents: W-2 from each employer, 1099 forms for any freelance/investment income, student loan interest statement (Form 1098-E)
  2. Choose how to file: Free File through IRS.gov (free for income under 9,000), TurboTax, H&R Block, or FreeTaxUSA (good paid option, low cost)
  3. Standard deduction vs itemized: Almost everyone under 35 should take the standard deduction (4,600 single in 2024) — it's simpler and usually larger than itemizing
  4. Common credits to look for: Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) for lower incomes, American Opportunity Credit for college tuition, student loan interest deduction
  5. File even if you think you don't owe: If any taxes were withheld from your paycheck, filing is how you get them back as a refund

A Refund Is Not a Bonus

Many people are excited to get a tax refund. But a large refund means you overpaid taxes throughout the year — you gave the government an interest-free loan. A refund of ,000 means you overpaid by 50/month all year. Adjusting your W-4 to get that money in each paycheck is almost always better.
