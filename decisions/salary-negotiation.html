How to Negotiate Your Salary

Every future raise, promotion, and job offer is often benchmarked from your current salary. Over 10 years at 3% annual raises, that ,000 gap becomes a 7,000 difference in cumulative earnings.

The Negotiation Most People Skip — and Why

Studies consistently show that most people do not negotiate their first job offer. The most common reasons: fear of seeming ungrateful, fear the offer will be rescinded, or simply not knowing how. None of these fears are justified in normal hiring situations.

  • Offers are almost never rescinded for negotiating — a company that rescinds an offer because you asked a professional salary question is a company you don't want to work for
  • Employers expect negotiation — most initial offers are intentionally below what the company is actually willing to pay
  • The worst realistic outcome is they say no and the original offer stands — you're exactly where you started

Before You Negotiate: Know Your Number

Walk in with a specific, researched number — not a vague feeling that you deserve more. Here's how to find yours:

  • Glassdoor and Levels.fyi — salary data by company, role, and location
  • LinkedIn Salary — role and industry data filtered by location
  • Bureau of Labor Statistics (bls.gov) — free government data on median wages by occupation
  • Talking to people in the field — the most accurate source. People are often more willing to share salary information than you'd expect, especially in professional communities
Your target range: Research the market rate for your role, experience level, and city. Your ask should be at the top third of that range — not the median. You're negotiating down from your ask, not up from theirs.

The Exact Words to Use

The most effective negotiation scripts are simple, confident, and non-confrontational. You're not demanding — you're having a professional conversation.

When They Make the Offer

Never accept on the spot. Always take time to review, even if you already know you'll take it:

Script: Buying Time to Respond

"Thank you so much — I'm really excited about this opportunity. Could I have until [specific date, 2–5 days] to review the offer and get back to you?"

Making Your Counter-Offer

Script: The Counter-Offer

"I'm very excited about this role and the team. Based on my research into market rates for this position in [city] and the experience I bring in [specific relevant skills], I was hoping we could get to [your number]. Is there flexibility there?"

Then stop talking. Silence is not your enemy here. Let them respond.

If They Say No or Can't Move on Base Salary

Script: Negotiating Other Components

"I understand — I appreciate you looking into it. If the base isn't flexible, would you be open to discussing [signing bonus / additional PTO / earlier performance review / remote flexibility]? I want to make this work."

What Else Is Negotiable Beyond Salary

Base salary is just one lever. If they truly can't move on salary, these are frequently negotiable:

  • Signing bonus — a one-time payment that doesn't affect base salary benchmarks for the company. Often easier for them to give than a higher base.
  • Start date — giving yourself an extra week or two can be valuable
  • Remote work flexibility — even one additional WFH day per week saves commute costs and time
  • Performance review timing — asking for a 6-month review instead of 12 creates an earlier opportunity for a raise
  • Additional PTO — one extra week of PTO has real monetary value
  • Professional development budget — courses, certifications, conference attendance
  • Title adjustment — a higher title can affect future offers from other companies

Negotiating a Raise in Your Current Job

The principles are similar, but the timing and framing matter more:

  • Time it right — after a win, after a performance review, after taking on new responsibilities. Not during a company downturn or right after a colleague was let go.
  • Document your value — build a list of specific accomplishments with numbers before the conversation. Revenue generated, costs saved, projects completed, metrics improved.
  • Know what you'd do if they say no — the strongest negotiating position is having an alternative. If you have another offer or are actively interviewing, your leverage is real. You don't have to say so explicitly, but it changes how you carry the conversation.
  • Ask for a specific number — "I was hoping we could discuss bringing my salary to " is more effective than "I was hoping for a raise."
The most important rule: Ask. The research is consistently clear — people who negotiate earn more over their careers than those who don't. The discomfort of one conversation is worth far more than you think.
