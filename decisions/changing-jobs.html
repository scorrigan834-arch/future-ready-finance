Should I Change Jobs?

Changing jobs is one of the most powerful financial moves available — and one of the most personal. Here is how to weigh the raise against everything else that matters.

Home › Guides › Should I Change Jobs?

The number that matters most: The average raise from staying put is 3–5% a year. The average raise from changing jobs is 10–20%. Over a career, strategic job changes can be worth hundreds of thousands of dollars — but timing and reasons matter.

Why Changing Jobs Often Pays More Than Staying

Internal raises are usually tied to small annual budgets and cost-of-living adjustments. When you switch companies, your new employer sets your salary based on current market rates and what it takes to win you — which is often well above what your current employer would ever give you to stay.

Beyond Salary: What Else to Weigh

Money matters, but a job change is never only about the paycheck. A higher salary at a toxic company or with no growth path can be a bad trade.

FactorQuestions to Ask
Total compensationHow do benefits, 401(k) match, equity, and bonus compare — not just base salary?
Growth trajectoryDoes this role open doors, or is it a lateral move with a raise attached?
StabilityIs the company financially healthy? What's the risk of layoffs?
Culture and managementWill you work for someone who develops you, or just extracts from you?
Work-life balanceDoes the higher pay come with much longer hours or constant stress?
Vesting you'd forfeitWould leaving cost you unvested 401(k) match or equity?

Signs It Might Be Time to Move

  • You've been denied a raise or promotion despite strong performance
  • Your pay has fallen behind market rate for your role
  • There's no clear path to growth where you are
  • You're learning nothing new and feel stagnant
  • The company or your team is unstable or poorly managed
  • Your mental health is suffering from the environment

Signs It Might Be Worth Staying

  • You're on a clear track to promotion or a raise soon
  • You're gaining valuable skills, mentorship, or experience
  • You have strong unvested equity or match worth waiting for
  • The culture, flexibility, or stability genuinely supports your life
  • A counteroffer could realistically close the pay gap (though counteroffers carry their own risks)
On counteroffers: If you get another offer and your current employer counters to keep you, be cautious. Studies suggest many who accept counteroffers leave within a year anyway — the underlying reasons you looked elsewhere usually remain. Use offers as information, not just leverage.

How to Change Jobs the Smart Way

  • Never quit before you have a signed offer — job searching is far easier while employed
  • Time it around your vesting and bonus — leaving right before a bonus pays out or equity vests can cost real money
  • Negotiate the new offer — the initial number is rarely the best they can do
  • Roll over your old 401(k) — don't cash it out and trigger taxes and penalties
  • Leave professionally — give notice, don't burn bridges, the industry is smaller than it seems
The bottom line: Loyalty to a company is rarely rewarded the way loyalty to your own growth is. Change jobs for the right reasons — growth, fit, and fair pay — not just to chase a number, and you'll build both a better career and a stronger financial future.
