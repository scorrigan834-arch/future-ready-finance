How Much Student Debt Is Too Much?

Understand how borrowing decisions shape your future flexibility — before you sign the promissory note.

Home › Guides › Student Debt

The Rule of Thumb: Total student debt should not exceed your expected starting annual salary in your field. Borrow $40,000 → aim to earn at least $40,000 in year one.

Why This Decision Matters So Much

Student debt is one of the few financial obligations that follows you for decades. Unlike credit card debt or a car loan, student loans are nearly impossible to discharge in bankruptcy. The amount you borrow today directly determines your monthly payment — and how much financial flexibility you'll have for everything else in your 20s and 30s.

The Debt-to-Income Framework

Debt vs Starting SalaryRisk Level
Below 1× starting salary✅ Manageable
1–1.5× starting salary⚠️ Stressful
Above 1.5× starting salary🚨 Dangerous

Example: Education Degree

Average starting salary for teachers: ~$40,000/year. If you borrow $60,000, your debt is 1.5× your income — placing you in the "stressful" range with likely $600–700/month in payments on a standard 10-year plan.

What Your Payments Will Actually Look Like

On a standard 10-year federal repayment plan, expect roughly $100–$120/month for every $10,000 you borrow:

Amount BorrowedMonthly Payment (est.)
$20,000~$200–240/month
$40,000~$400–480/month
$60,000~$600–720/month
$80,000~$800–960/month

Questions to Ask Before Borrowing

  • What is the median starting salary for graduates of this specific program at this school?
  • What is the total cost of my degree, including all four years?
  • Have I maximized free money first — grants, scholarships, work-study?
  • What will my monthly payment be on the day I graduate?
  • Can I afford to rent an apartment and make this payment at the same time?
Alternatives worth exploring: Community college for the first two years, in-state public universities, trade programs, employer tuition assistance, and income-driven repayment plans all change the math significantly.

The Opportunity Cost You're Not Thinking About

Every dollar of student loan payment is a dollar not going toward building wealth. A person paying $500/month in student loans for 10 years spends $60,000 that could have gone into retirement savings or a home down payment. The total cost of borrowing is always more than the principal.
