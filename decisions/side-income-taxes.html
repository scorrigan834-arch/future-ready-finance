Side Hustle Taxes: What Every Freelancer Needs to Know

Freelance income, gig work, selling online, tutoring, photography — if you're earning money outside a traditional job, the tax rules are completely different. Here's what no one tells you.

Home › Guides › Side Hustle Taxes

The most expensive mistake freelancers make: Spending all their income and then discovering they owe 25–30% of it to the IRS in April. Self-employment taxes are real, they're significant, and they're your responsibility to track and pay — no one withholds them for you.

Why Side Income Is Taxed Differently

When you work a traditional job (W-2), your employer withholds income taxes and pays half of your Social Security and Medicare taxes (7.65%) on your behalf. When you're self-employed, you pay all of it yourself.

Self-Employment Tax: The Number That Surprises Everyone

Self-employment tax = 15.3% of net self-employment income
This covers: Social Security (12.4%) + Medicare (2.9%)

Plus your regular income tax on top of that.

Example: 0,000 in freelance income
Self-employment tax: ~,826 (15.3% × 92.35% of income)
Income tax (22% bracket): ~,400
Total owed: ~,226 — or about 36% of your income

Small consolation: you can deduct half the self-employment tax from your income, which slightly reduces the income tax portion.

The 00 Rule

If you earn 00 or more in net self-employment income in a calendar year, you are required to file a tax return and pay self-employment tax — even if you also have a regular job. This threshold is low by design. A single freelance project, a few Etsy sales, or a month of DoorDash can cross it easily.

Quarterly Estimated Taxes: Your New Deadline Calendar

W-2 employees have taxes withheld from every paycheck. Self-employed people pay estimated taxes four times per year instead. Miss these and the IRS charges underpayment penalties on top of what you owe.

Payment PeriodDue Date
January 1 – March 31April 15
April 1 – May 31June 17
June 1 – August 31September 16
September 1 – December 31January 15 (following year)

You pay through IRS Direct Pay (free at irs.gov) or by mailing a Form 1040-ES voucher with a check. Set calendar reminders — these deadlines do not move.

Safe harbor rule: To avoid underpayment penalties, pay at least 100% of last year's total tax bill across your four quarterly payments (110% if your income was over 50,000). This gives you a penalty-free buffer even if your income fluctuates.

What You Can Deduct

The major advantage of self-employment is that legitimate business expenses reduce your taxable income — meaning you pay taxes on profit, not gross revenue. Keep receipts and records for everything.

The Simple System: How to Manage Side Income Taxes

  1. Open a separate checking account for side income — keep business money completely separate from personal money
  2. Set aside 25–30% of every payment — immediately transfer to a dedicated tax savings account. Don't touch it.
  3. Track every expense with an app — Wave (free) or QuickBooks Self-Employed (5/month) sync to your bank and categorize expenses automatically
  4. Pay quarterly estimates — use irs.gov/payments to pay each quarter
  5. File Schedule C with your annual return — this is where you report self-employment income and deductions

When to Consider an LLC

A sole proprietorship (just you, no legal entity formed) is the default for most freelancers and requires nothing to set up. An LLC (Limited Liability Company) provides personal asset protection — if someone sues your business, your personal bank account and home are protected. Consider forming one when:

  • You have significant revenue (typically 0,000+/year) or growing client contracts
  • Your work carries liability risk (consulting, photography at events, physical services)
  • You want to appear more professional to clients and sign contracts as a business
  • You're ready to open a business bank account and keep finances fully separate

An LLC does not change how you're taxed by default — you still file Schedule C unless you elect to be taxed as an S-Corp, which makes sense at higher income levels.
