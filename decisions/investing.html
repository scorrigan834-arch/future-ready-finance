Should I Start Investing Now?

See why time is your most powerful wealth-building advantage — and why waiting is more expensive than most people realize.

Home › Guides › Investing Early

The short answer: Yes. Even small amounts, as early as possible. Time in the market matters more than the amount you start with.

The Power of Compounding

Compounding means your investment returns generate their own returns over time. The longer your money is invested, the more this effect accelerates. The math strongly favors starting early.

Starting at 22 vs. Starting at 32

Invest $200/month starting at 22 with 7% average annual return → ~$525,000 by age 62.

Wait until 32 to start the same $200/month → ~$243,000 by age 62.

Waiting 10 years costs over $280,000 — despite only contributing $24,000 more in total dollars.

But What About My Debt?

SituationWhat to Do
High-interest debt (8%+)Pay this down aggressively first
Employer 401(k) match availableAlways contribute enough to get the full match
Student loans (3–6%)Consider doing both: minimum loan payments + invest
No emergency fundBuild 3 months of expenses before investing aggressively

Where to Start

  • Workplace 401(k): Contribute at least enough to get any employer match. An employer match is an instant 50–100% return on your money.
  • Roth IRA: After-tax dollars now; withdrawals in retirement are tax-free. Best for younger people who expect their income to rise.
  • Index funds: Low-cost funds that track the broad market. Look for expense ratios below 0.20%.

How Much Should I Invest?

A common target is 15% of gross income toward retirement, including any employer match. If that's not possible, start with 3% and increase by 1% each time you get a raise. The habit matters as much as the amount.

The biggest mistake: Waiting until you "have enough money" to start. There is no perfect time. Starting small and consistent outperforms starting large and late — every time.
