How Much House Can I Afford?

Banks will approve you for more than you should borrow. Here's how to find the number that works for your actual life — not just your credit score.

Home › Guides › How Much House Can I Afford

The most dangerous number in homebuying: The pre-approval amount. Lenders qualify you based on whether you can make the payment — not whether doing so leaves room for retirement, savings, emergencies, or enjoying your life. The bank's number and your real budget are almost always very different.

The Two Rules Every Buyer Needs

Rule 1 — The 28% Rule (Front-End Ratio)

Total monthly housing costs — mortgage principal, interest, property taxes, and homeowner's insurance (PITI) — should not exceed 28% of gross monthly income.

28% Rule — Real Example

Gross income: 5,000/year = ,083/month
28% of ,083 = ,983 maximum PITI

At a 7% 30-year mortgage, ,983/month supports a loan of about 98,000.
With a 20% down payment → maximum home price of roughly 72,000.

Rule 2 — The 36% Rule (Back-End / Debt-to-Income)

Total monthly debt payments — mortgage plus all other debts (student loans, car, credit cards) — should not exceed 36% of gross monthly income. Lenders allow up to 43%, but 36% gives you breathing room.

36% Rule — Why Your Other Debts Shrink Your Budget

Gross income: ,083/month
36% = ,550 maximum total debt payments

If you have 50/month in student loans and a car payment → only ,900 left for housing.

Your existing debts directly reduce how much house you can afford. Pay down high balances before buying if possible.

The Quick Affordability Check

  1. Gross annual income × 3 = rough ceiling for home price (3× income rule). On 0k income: ~40k max if you carry no other debt.
  2. Gross monthly income × 0.28 = maximum PITI payment. This is your housing budget ceiling.
  3. Use a mortgage calculator at current rates to find the loan amount that produces that payment.
  4. Add your down payment to that loan amount → your maximum home price.
  5. Confirm you still have 3–6 months of expenses in savings after down payment and closing costs.
  6. If the answer doesn't work — you need more down payment, lower debt load, or higher income before buying.
The question most first-time buyers don't ask: "What happens to our budget if one of us loses income, needs a major medical procedure, or if the HVAC dies?" A home you can barely afford in ideal conditions is a home that owns you — not the other way around.
