Couples and Money: The Hard Conversations

Beyond joint accounts and budgets lie the conversations most couples avoid — prenups, what happens in a breakup, and navigating very different incomes. Avoiding them doesn't make them go away.

Home › Guides › The Hard Conversations

Why this matters: Money is one of the leading sources of conflict in relationships and a top cited factor in divorce. The couples who do best aren't the ones who avoid these topics — they're the ones who talk about them openly, early, and without shame.

Prenuptial Agreements: Not Just for the Wealthy

A prenuptial agreement (prenup) is a legal contract signed before marriage that defines how assets and debts are handled if the marriage ends. The word carries a stigma, but a prenup is fundamentally a planning tool — like insurance you hope never to use.

What a Prenup Can Do

  • Protect premarital assets: Keep what you each brought into the marriage separate
  • Shield one partner from the other's debt: Especially relevant if one person carries significant student loans or business debt
  • Protect a business or inheritance: Keep a family business or expected inheritance out of marital property
  • Clarify expectations: Define financial roles and reduce uncertainty
  • Protect children from a prior relationship: Ensure assets are preserved for existing children

What a Prenup Generally Cannot Do

  • Decide child custody or child support (courts decide these based on the child's best interest at the time)
  • Include anything illegal or grossly unfair (a court may throw out a one-sided agreement)
  • Govern non-financial matters like household chores in an enforceable way
Who should consider one: Anyone with significant assets, a business, expected inheritance, large debts, children from a prior relationship, or a meaningful income gap. For many couples with few assets, it may not be necessary — but the conversation itself is valuable.

How to Approach the Conversation

Frame it as mutual protection, not distrust. Bring it up early — not weeks before the wedding. Each partner should have their own attorney review it, and full financial disclosure from both sides is essential for it to hold up. A prenup signed under pressure or without disclosure can be invalidated.

What Happens Financially in a Breakup or Divorce

No one enters a relationship planning for it to end, but understanding the financial mechanics protects you either way. The rules differ significantly depending on whether you're married.

For Unmarried Couples

Living together without marriage offers little automatic legal protection. If you buy property together, mix finances, or one partner financially supports the other, untangling it can be messy with no legal framework to fall back on.

  • Keep records: Document who paid for what, especially large shared purchases
  • Title things clearly: Whose name is on the lease, the car, the deed matters enormously
  • Consider a cohabitation agreement: A written agreement about shared finances and property for unmarried partners
  • Be cautious with joint accounts: Either party can typically drain a joint account, and you may be liable for joint debt

For Married Couples

ConceptWhat It Means
Community propertyIn some states, most assets and debts acquired during marriage are split 50/50 regardless of who earned them
Equitable distributionIn most states, marital property is divided "fairly" — which doesn't always mean equally
Separate propertyAssets owned before marriage or received as a gift/inheritance often stay with the original owner — if kept separate
ComminglingWhen separate property gets mixed with marital property (like depositing an inheritance into a joint account), it can lose its separate status
Spousal supportOne spouse may pay the other support after divorce, especially after long marriages with income gaps
The protective habit: Maintaining some financial independence within a partnership — your own credit history, some accounts in your own name, and knowledge of your shared finances — isn't a sign of distrust. It's basic security that protects you whether the relationship lasts forever or not.

Navigating Very Different Incomes

When one partner earns substantially more than the other, money dynamics get complicated. Handled poorly, income gaps breed resentment and power imbalances. Handled well, they're just a logistical detail.

The Proportional Approach

Instead of splitting shared costs 50/50, split them in proportion to income. If one partner earns 70% of the household income, they cover 70% of shared expenses. This feels fairer than equal splits when incomes differ widely.

Protect the Lower Earner's Autonomy

The lower-earning partner should still have their own money to spend without justification. A system where one person controls all the money creates an unhealthy power dynamic, regardless of who earns more.

Don't Tie Contribution to Worth

Income is not a measure of a person's value to the relationship. The partner earning less may contribute more in caregiving, household management, or emotional labor. Healthy couples recognize all forms of contribution.

Plan for the Lower Earner's Future

If one partner steps back from work — for caregiving, for example — their retirement savings and career can suffer long-term. Couples should consciously fund the lower earner's retirement (a spousal IRA, for instance) so one person's sacrifice doesn't become their long-term financial vulnerability.

The Proportional Contribution Formula

Add both incomes for total household income. Divide each person's income by that total to get their percentage. Apply that percentage to shared expenses.

The Conversations Worth Having Early

  • What does money mean to each of us? (Security? Freedom? Status?)
  • What debts and assets are each of us bringing in?
  • How do we want to handle shared vs separate money?
  • What are our individual and shared financial goals?
  • How will we handle it if our incomes change dramatically?
  • What's our plan if one of us wants to step back from work?
  • How do we each feel about a prenup or cohabitation agreement?
The bottom line: These conversations feel uncomfortable precisely because they matter. Couples who can talk openly about prenups, breakups, and income gaps build relationships on honesty rather than avoidance — and they're far better prepared for whatever comes.
