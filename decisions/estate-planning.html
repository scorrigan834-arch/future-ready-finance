Estate Planning Basics for Young Adults

It's not just for the wealthy or the old. A few simple documents protect you and the people you love.

Guides › Estate Planning

Estate planning sounds like something only wealthy retirees need. It's not. Even young adults with modest savings benefit from a few basic documents that protect them and the people they care about. Here's the simple version.

What estate planning actually means

At its core, estate planning is just deciding in advance what happens to your money and belongings — and who makes decisions for you — if you can't. It's less about wealth and more about removing burden and confusion from the people you'd leave behind in a hard moment.

The documents worth having

📄 A Will

Names who gets your belongings and, if you have children, who would care for them. Without one, the state decides using default rules that may not match your wishes.

⚖️ Power of Attorney

Names someone to handle your finances if you're unable to — due to an accident or illness. Without it, your family may face a court process just to pay your bills.

🏥 Healthcare Directive

Also called a living will. States your medical wishes and names someone to make health decisions if you can't speak for yourself. Spares loved ones agonizing guesses.

👤 Beneficiaries

The people you name on accounts like a 401(k), IRA, or life insurance. These designations often override a will, so keeping them current is one of the simplest, most important steps.

Beneficiary designations are the easiest win. Your retirement accounts and insurance policies pass directly to whoever you've named as beneficiary — no will required, no court. Take five minutes to check that these are filled in and up to date, especially after big life changes. It's the highest-impact, lowest-effort piece of estate planning.

Why young adults shouldn't skip it

  • Accidents happen at any age — these documents are about the unexpected, not just old age.
  • It protects the people around you from legal and financial headaches during a crisis.
  • Digital lives need a plan too — accounts, photos, and assets that someone may need to access.
  • It's cheaper and simpler than you think — basic documents don't require a fortune or a law degree.

How to start

You don't need to do everything at once. Start with the easiest, highest-impact step: check the beneficiaries on your retirement and insurance accounts today. From there, simple will and power-of-attorney documents are widely available, and an attorney makes sense as your life and assets grow more complex. A little planning now is a real gift to your future self and the people you love.
