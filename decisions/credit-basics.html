How Credit Scores Work — and How to Build One

Your credit score is a number that follows you for decades. It affects your rent, your car loan, your mortgage, and sometimes even your job. Here's exactly how it works.

Home › Guides › Credit Basics

Why this matters at 16: You can start building credit before you need it. The people who get the best mortgage rates at 28 are the ones who opened a credit card responsibly at 18. Credit history takes time — start early.

What a Credit Score Actually Is

A credit score is a 3-digit number (300–850) that summarizes how reliably you've repaid money you've borrowed. Lenders use it to decide whether to lend to you — and at what interest rate. The higher your score, the lower the rate you'll be offered, which translates to thousands of dollars of real savings over a lifetime.

  • Payment History (35%): Do you pay on time? One missed payment can drop your score 60–110 points. This is the single most important factor.
  • Amounts Owed / Credit Utilization (30%): What percentage of your available credit are you using? Below 30% is good. Below 10% is excellent. Using 80% of your credit limit hurts significantly.
  • Length of Credit History (15%): How long have your accounts been open? Older accounts help. This is why you should never close your first credit card.
  • Credit Mix (10%): Do you have a variety of credit types (credit card, auto loan, student loan)? A mix helps slightly.
  • New Credit (10%): How many new accounts or hard inquiries have you had recently? Opening many accounts in a short period hurts temporarily.

How to Build Credit From Zero

If you have no credit history, here are the most reliable paths to building it:

Option 1: Secured Credit Card

A secured card requires a deposit (usually 00–500) that becomes your credit limit. You use it like a normal card and pay it off monthly. After 12–18 months of on-time payments, most cards upgrade you to a regular (unsecured) card and return your deposit. This is the most common first step.

Option 2: Become an Authorized User

If a parent or trusted family member has good credit and a credit card in good standing, they can add you as an authorized user. Their payment history on that card appears on your credit report. You don't even need to use the card — just being listed builds your history.

Option 3: Student Credit Card

Many issuers offer credit cards specifically for college students with no credit history — lower limits, no annual fee, and easier approval. Discover it Student, Chase Freedom Student, and Capital One Journey are common starting points.

Option 4: Credit-Builder Loan

Some credit unions offer credit-builder loans specifically designed to help people establish credit. You make payments on a small loan (00–1,500), and the funds are released to you when the loan is paid off. You build credit history while saving money.

The Rules for Using a Credit Card Responsibly

The Only Three Rules You Need

1. Pay the full balance every month — never just the minimum. The minimum payment is designed to keep you in debt for years.

2. Keep utilization below 30% — if your limit is ,000, never carry more than 00 on the card at any time.

3. Treat it like a debit card — only charge what you already have the cash to pay. A credit card is not extra money.

What Is APR and Why It Matters

APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the interest rate charged on your balance if you don't pay it off in full each month. Most credit cards charge 20–30% APR.

If you carry a ,000 balance on a 24% APR card and only make minimum payments, you'll pay approximately 30 in interest and take over 5 years to pay it off — for a ,000 purchase. This is how credit card debt destroys financial progress.

If you pay your full balance every month, your APR is irrelevant — you never pay interest. The APR only matters if you carry a balance.

Check your score for free: You can check your credit score for free through Credit Karma, Experian, or directly through many bank apps. Checking your own score is a "soft inquiry" and does not hurt your score at all.

What Hurts Your Credit Score Most

  • Missing a payment — even one missed payment stays on your credit report for 7 years
  • High utilization — using more than 30–50% of your available credit
  • Applying for too many cards at once — each application creates a hard inquiry that temporarily lowers your score
  • Closing old accounts — shortens your credit history and reduces available credit (raising utilization)
  • Collections — unpaid bills sent to collections (medical, phone, utilities) severely damage your score
