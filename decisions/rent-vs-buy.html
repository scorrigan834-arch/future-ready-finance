Should I Rent or Buy?

One of the most debated questions in personal finance — and one of the most misunderstood. Here's the real framework so you can answer it for your actual situation.

Home › Guides › Rent vs Buy

The myth to retire first: "Renting is throwing money away." This is one of the most repeated — and least accurate — pieces of financial advice. Rent buys you housing, flexibility, and freedom from maintenance. A mortgage buys equity, but only after paying interest, taxes, insurance, and repairs for years. Neither is automatically better. The right answer depends entirely on your timeline, market, and financial situation.

What You're Actually Comparing

The real comparison isn't rent payment vs mortgage payment. It's the total cost of each path over the time you plan to stay — including everything beyond the mortgage that buying requires.

The Real Monthly Cost of a 50,000 Home

Mortgage (30yr at 7%): ~,862/month
Property taxes (1.2% avg): ~50/month
Homeowner's insurance: ~50/month
Maintenance budget (1%/yr): ~92/month

True monthly cost: ~,654 — not ,862.

Plus you need ~0,500 upfront (20% down + closing costs) before making a single payment. Many buyers focus on the mortgage payment and are blindsided by everything else.

The Breakeven Timeline — The Number Nobody Calculates

Buying has massive upfront costs. It takes years before those costs are recovered through equity and appreciation. The point where buying becomes cheaper than renting the same home is the breakeven point — typically 5–7 years in most markets.

Under 3 Years

Rent. Upfront costs almost never recover this fast. Even in rising markets, transaction costs eat your gains. Don't buy a home you plan to leave in 2 years.

3–5 Years

It depends. Run your specific numbers. Buying could work in fast-appreciating markets or if you get a great deal — but it's not automatic.

5–7+ Years

Buying starts making increasing financial sense, especially if you can afford it without straining your budget or emergency fund.

10+ Years

In most markets, buying wins over a long horizon — equity builds, the mortgage stays fixed while rents rise, and the asset appreciates.

The Price-to-Rent Ratio: A Quick Market Sanity Check

Take the home's purchase price and divide by annual rent for a comparable home. This tells you how expensive buying is relative to renting in your specific market.

The Honest Case for Each

Rent When...

  • You may move in the next 3–5 years
  • Your income is variable or new
  • You don't have 20% down + closing costs + emergency fund
  • Your local price-to-rent ratio is above 20
  • You're still learning the city or neighborhood
  • You value flexibility over roots right now

Buy When...

  • You're confident you'll stay 5+ years
  • You have stable income and a solid emergency fund post-purchase
  • You have 20% down + closing costs saved
  • Monthly PITI is under 28% of gross income
  • You want to customize your space and build roots
  • Comparable rents are near or above what a mortgage would cost

The Opportunity Cost Nobody Mentions

A 0,000 down payment invested in a diversified portfolio at 7% average annual return grows to approximately 74,000 over 20 years. That doesn't mean don't buy — home equity also builds wealth. But it means the true cost comparison must include what that capital would otherwise earn, not just what you avoid paying in rent.

The honest answer is that both renting and buying can be great financial decisions — and both can be terrible ones — depending entirely on your timeline, market, and financial readiness.
