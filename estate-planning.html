Estate Planning: Wills, Trusts, and Protecting Your Family

Estate planning is not about dying. It's about making sure the people you care about are protected — and that your wishes are followed — if you're ever unable to speak for yourself.

Home › Guides › Estate Planning

Who needs estate planning: Anyone with assets, dependents, strong preferences about medical care, or people they want to protect. That's not just retirees — it's anyone over 18 with a bank account, a job, or a family.

Why Most People Avoid This — And Why That's a Mistake

Estate planning feels like confronting mortality, so most people delay it indefinitely. The result: if something happens, the government and courts decide who gets your assets, who makes your medical decisions, and who raises your children — not you. A basic estate plan takes a few hours and costs far less than most people think.

The Last Will and Testament

A will is a legal document that directs where your assets go after you die. Without one, your state's intestacy laws determine this — which may not reflect your wishes at all.

  • What it covers: Distribution of assets not covered by beneficiary designations, guardianship of minor children, and appointment of an executor (the person who carries out your wishes)
  • What it does not cover: Assets that pass by beneficiary designation (retirement accounts, life insurance, jointly titled property) or assets held in a trust — these pass outside the will
  • Probate: A will must go through probate — a public court process that can take months to years and cost 3–7% of the estate. A trust avoids this.
  • Most important if you have children: The guardianship designation in a will is the only way to legally name who raises your children if both parents die

The Single Most Important Estate Planning Step for Parents

If you have minor children and do only one estate planning document, make it a will — specifically to name a guardian. Without it, a court will decide who raises your children. The court will try to choose well, but it may not align with your values, relationships, or wishes.

Revocable Living Trust

A revocable living trust holds your assets during your lifetime and distributes them after death — without going through probate. You remain in control of the trust while you're alive and can change it at any time.

  • Advantages: Avoids probate (private, faster, and cheaper), allows for smooth transfer of assets, can provide asset management if you become incapacitated
  • Disadvantages: More expensive to create than a will (,000–3,000+ with an attorney vs 00–500 for a will), requires "funding" — you must actually transfer assets into the trust for it to work
  • Who benefits most: People with real estate in multiple states, significant assets, blended families, or a desire for privacy
  • Still need a will: Even with a trust, you need a "pour-over will" to catch any assets not transferred to the trust

Health Care Proxy (Health Care Power of Attorney)

A health care proxy designates someone to make medical decisions on your behalf if you are unable to make them yourself. This is one of the most important documents for anyone over 18.

  • What it does: Gives your chosen person (your "agent" or "proxy") legal authority to speak with doctors and make healthcare decisions based on your known wishes
  • Without one: Even your spouse may have limited legal authority to make decisions in some states. Parents lose automatic decision-making authority for children once they turn 18.
  • Choose carefully: Your health care proxy should understand your values, be able to make difficult decisions under stress, and be available in a crisis
  • College students: Once your child turns 18, you legally may not be able to make medical decisions for them or even access their medical information without a HIPAA authorization and health care proxy

Advance Directive (Living Will)

An advance directive documents your wishes about end-of-life medical care — specifically what interventions you want or do not want if you are terminally ill or permanently unconscious.

  • What it addresses: Ventilator use, feeding tubes, resuscitation (DNR/DNI orders), organ donation, and other life-sustaining treatments
  • How it works with a health care proxy: The advance directive documents your wishes; the health care proxy applies them and fills in gaps when situations arise that the document doesn't specifically address
  • State-specific: Forms vary by state. Use your state's official form or work with an estate planning attorney.

Financial Power of Attorney (Durable POA)

A durable power of attorney designates someone to manage your financial affairs if you become incapacitated — paying bills, managing investments, filing taxes, and handling property transactions.

  • Durable: Remains in effect if you become incapacitated (a non-durable POA terminates upon incapacity — which is usually the wrong choice)
  • Springing vs immediate: A springing POA only activates upon incapacity. An immediate POA is active from signing. Springing sounds more protective but can create delays in emergencies.
  • Without one: A court may need to appoint a conservator to manage your finances — a public, expensive, time-consuming process

Beneficiary Designations: The Easiest Win Most People Miss

Beneficiary designations on retirement accounts (401k, IRA), life insurance policies, and bank accounts override your will entirely. The person named on the form gets the money — regardless of what your will says.

  • Review after every major life event: marriage, divorce, birth of a child, death of a beneficiary
  • Name contingent beneficiaries: If your primary beneficiary dies before you and there's no contingent beneficiary named, the account goes through probate
  • Minor children as beneficiaries: Minor children cannot directly receive large sums. Name a trust as beneficiary, or the court will manage the money until they turn 18
  • Check your accounts today: Many people have ex-spouses, deceased relatives, or parents listed as beneficiaries on accounts they opened decades ago

The Estate Planning Starter Checklist

✓ Last will and testament (with guardian named if you have children)
✓ Health care proxy / health care power of attorney
✓ Advance directive / living will
✓ Durable financial power of attorney
✓ Beneficiary designations reviewed on all accounts
✓ Consider a revocable living trust if you have real estate or significant assets
