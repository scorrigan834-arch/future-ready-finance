Insurance and Risk Management

Insurance is not about what you think will happen. It's about protecting against what you can't afford to have happen. Here's what you need, when you need it, and why.

The purpose of insurance: Transfer financial risk you cannot absorb to an insurance company in exchange for a predictable premium. You insure against catastrophic losses — not minor inconveniences.

Life Insurance

Life insurance replaces your income for people who depend on it financially. If no one depends on your income — no spouse, no children, no one you support — you likely don't need it yet.

Term Life Insurance

Term life provides a death benefit for a fixed period (10, 20, or 30 years) in exchange for a level annual premium. It's the simplest, most affordable, and most appropriate type for most people.

  • When you need it: When someone depends on your income — typically when you have a spouse, children, or a mortgage
  • How much: A common guideline is 10–12× your annual income. For a 0,000 earner that means 00,000–40,000 of coverage
  • How long: Long enough to cover your longest financial obligation — usually until children are grown or a mortgage is paid off
  • Cost: A healthy 30-year-old can typically get 00,000 of 20-year term coverage for 5–35/month

Whole Life and Universal Life Insurance

Permanent life insurance combines a death benefit with a cash value component. These policies are significantly more expensive than term and are frequently oversold. For most people under 40, term life insurance is the appropriate choice.

Disability Insurance (DI)

Disability insurance is the most underowned and undervalued insurance in personal finance. Your income is your most valuable financial asset — disability insurance protects it if illness or injury prevents you from working.

  • The risk: You are statistically far more likely to become disabled before retirement than to die before retirement. One in four workers will experience a disability lasting 90+ days during their career.
  • Short-term disability: Covers 60–70% of your income for a short period (typically 3–6 months). Many employers provide this.
  • Long-term disability: Kicks in after short-term expires. Should cover at least 60% of pre-disability income until age 65.
  • Own-occupation vs any-occupation: Own-occupation policies pay if you can't do your specific job. Any-occupation policies only pay if you can't do any job at all. Own-occupation is significantly more valuable.
  • Elimination period: The waiting period before benefits begin (30, 60, or 90 days). A longer elimination period lowers the premium — but requires a larger emergency fund to bridge the gap.
Check your employer first: Many employers provide group disability coverage. Understand what you have before buying individual coverage. Group coverage often has gaps — particularly for high earners, the self-employed, or those who change jobs frequently.

Long-Term Care Insurance (LTC)

Long-term care insurance covers the cost of assistance with daily living activities — bathing, eating, dressing — due to aging, chronic illness, or cognitive decline. These costs are not covered by Medicare for extended periods and can be financially devastating without coverage.

  • The cost of care: The median annual cost of a private nursing home room in the US is approximately 00,000. A 3-year stay — the average — costs 00,000 or more.
  • When to consider it: Typically purchased in your mid-50s to early 60s. Earlier may mean lower premiums, but you pay them longer. Later may mean higher premiums or uninsurability.
  • Hybrid policies: Life insurance policies with LTC riders combine death benefit with long-term care coverage — if you don't need LTC, the benefit passes to heirs.
  • Who needs it: Those without sufficient assets to self-insure (00,000+), those without a family caregiving plan, and those with family history of dementia or extended illness.

Liability and Umbrella Insurance

Liability insurance protects your assets from lawsuits. As you accumulate wealth, the exposure grows — and standard auto and homeowner policies have liability limits that may not be enough.

What's Already Covered

  • Auto liability: Required in most states. Covers damage you cause to others in a car accident. Most policies carry 00,000–00,000 of coverage.
  • Homeowner/renter liability: Covers injuries or damage that occur on your property. Typically 00,000–00,000.

Umbrella Insurance

An umbrella policy provides an additional –5 million of liability coverage above your existing auto and home policies. It's one of the most cost-effective insurance purchases available — typically 50–300/year for million of additional coverage.

  • Who needs it: Anyone with meaningful assets to protect, a swimming pool or trampoline, teenage drivers, a dog, or a side business
  • When to get it: Once your net worth exceeds 00,000 or you have assets you couldn't afford to lose in a lawsuit

Insurance by Life Stage

Life StagePriority Coverage
High School / CollegeHealth insurance (through parents or school), renters insurance if living off campus
Early Career (single)Health, disability (check employer), renters/auto, begin evaluating life insurance needs
Early Career (partnered/family)All above + term life insurance for income replacement, review beneficiaries
Establishing (30s)All above + umbrella policy as assets grow, review disability coverage adequacy
Mid-Career (40s–50s)All above + begin evaluating long-term care options, review life insurance needs
The rule of thumb: Insure what you can't afford to replace or absorb. Don't insure minor risks (extended warranties, credit card insurance, flight insurance). Do insure catastrophic risks (death, disability, liability, long-term care).
