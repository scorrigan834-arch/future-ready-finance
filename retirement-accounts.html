Roth vs Traditional vs HSA: Which Account Should I Use?

Three of the most powerful tax-advantaged accounts available to you — and most people only understand one of them. Here's how all three work and how to use them together.

Home › Guides › Retirement Accounts

The key insight: The difference between these accounts is not what you invest in — it's when you pay taxes. Getting this right can mean tens of thousands of dollars more in retirement.

The Core Difference: When Do You Pay Taxes?

Traditional 401(k) and IRA

Traditional accounts let you deduct contributions from your taxable income today. You get a tax break now, and pay taxes when you withdraw the money in retirement.

  • Best for: People who are in a higher tax bracket now than they expect to be in retirement
  • 2024 contribution limits: 401(k) — 3,000 (0,500 if 50+) | IRA — ,000 (,000 if 50+)
  • Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs): You must start withdrawing at age 73 whether you need the money or not
  • Early withdrawal penalty: 10% penalty plus taxes if you withdraw before age 59½ (with exceptions)

Traditional Example

You earn 5,000 and contribute ,000 to a Traditional IRA. Your taxable income drops to 9,000, saving you roughly ,320 in taxes today (at 22% bracket). In retirement, you withdraw the money and pay taxes then — ideally at a lower rate.

Roth 401(k) and Roth IRA

Roth accounts are funded with after-tax dollars — you pay taxes on the money before it goes in. But everything that grows inside the account, and all withdrawals in retirement, are completely tax-free.

  • Best for: People who are in a lower tax bracket now than they expect to be in retirement — which describes most young earners
  • Roth IRA income limits: In 2024, you can contribute the full amount if your income is under 46,000 (single) or 30,000 (married filing jointly)
  • No RMDs: Roth IRAs have no required withdrawals, making them excellent for wealth transfer
  • Contributions (not earnings) can be withdrawn anytime penalty-free: This makes a Roth IRA a more flexible emergency backup than a Traditional IRA

Roth Example — Why Young Earners Win

You're 24, in the 22% tax bracket, and put ,000/year into a Roth IRA for 40 years. At 7% average return, that grows to roughly .27 million — and every dollar of it is tax-free in retirement. Had you invested in a Traditional IRA, you'd owe income tax on every withdrawal.

Roth vs Traditional: How to Decide

Choose Roth If...

  • You're early in your career and in a lower tax bracket
  • You expect your income (and tax rate) to rise significantly
  • You want tax-free income in retirement
  • You want flexibility to access contributions if needed
  • You want to leave money to heirs tax-free

Choose Traditional If...

  • You're in a high tax bracket now and expect lower income in retirement
  • You need the tax deduction today to reduce your taxable income
  • Your employer only offers Traditional 401(k) matching
  • You're over 50 and prioritizing near-term tax savings
  • You're in a state with high income tax that might be lower in retirement
For most people under 35: Roth wins. You're likely in the lowest tax bracket you'll ever be in. Pay the taxes now, let it grow tax-free for 30–40 years, and never pay taxes on that growth again.

The HSA: The Most Powerful Account Most People Ignore

A Health Savings Account (HSA) is only available if you have a qualifying High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP). But if you do, it's arguably the best tax-advantaged account in existence — because it's the only account that gives you three tax benefits simultaneously.

The HSA Triple Tax Advantage

1. Contributions are pre-tax — reduces your taxable income today, just like a Traditional 401(k)

2. Growth is tax-free — invest the money and it grows without being taxed

3. Withdrawals for medical expenses are tax-free — forever, at any age, for any qualifying medical cost

No other account gives you all three. A 401(k) gives you 1 and 2. A Roth gives you 2 and 3. The HSA gives you all three — if the money is used for medical expenses.

HSA Key Details

  • 2024 contribution limits: ,150 (individual) | ,300 (family) | +,000 if 55+
  • Funds roll over every year — this is not a "use it or lose it" account like an FSA
  • After age 65: Withdrawals for non-medical expenses are taxed like a Traditional IRA — making the HSA a backup retirement account
  • Invest your HSA: Most people leave HSA funds in cash. Investing them in index funds lets the triple tax advantage compound over decades
  • Save your medical receipts: There's no time limit on reimbursing yourself. Pay out-of-pocket now, let the HSA grow invested, and reimburse yourself decades later — tax-free

The Optimal Order of Operations

If you have access to all three account types, here's the sequence most financial educators recommend:

  1. 401(k) up to the employer match — always capture 100% of free match money first
  2. Max your HSA — triple tax advantage beats everything else dollar-for-dollar
  3. Max your Roth IRA — ,000/year, tax-free forever
  4. Return to 401(k) — contribute beyond the match up to the annual limit
  5. Taxable brokerage account — after maxing tax-advantaged accounts
Reality check: Most people can't max all of these at once — and that's okay. The order matters more than the amount. Even 0/month in a Roth IRA at 22 is worth more than 00/month in a taxable account at 40.
