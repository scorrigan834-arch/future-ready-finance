How Do I Evaluate a Job Offer?

Salary is just one number. Total compensation — benefits, retirement match, health coverage, and cost of living — is what actually determines how far your money goes.

Home › Guides › Evaluating a Job Offer

The core mistake: Comparing job offers by salary alone is like comparing apartments by rent alone — you're ignoring everything that changes the real number. Always compare total compensation packages, not just base pay.

What Total Compensation Actually Includes

When a company says they're offering you 5,000, that's only base salary. Your true annual compensation can be 0,000–25,000 higher — or lower — depending on what else is in the package.

The 401(k) Match: Your Highest-Return Investment

A 401(k) employer match is an instant, guaranteed 50–100% return on your money. It's the first thing to understand in any offer. But the details matter:

  • Match percentage: "We match 50% of contributions up to 6% of salary" means if you earn 0,000 and contribute 6% (,200), the company adds ,100. That's ,100 of free money.
  • Vesting schedule: Some employers make you stay 3–5 years before the match is truly "yours." If you leave before vesting, you forfeit the match. A 3-year cliff vesting at a company you leave in 18 months = /bin/sh match value.
  • Match cap: Companies typically match up to a percentage of your salary. Know the cap so you know the maximum free money available.
Example: Company A offers 0,000 with a 4% match and pays 80% of your health premium. Company B offers 5,000 with no match and you pay full health costs. Company A's total value is likely higher — by potentially ,000–2,000 — despite the lower salary.

Cost of Living: Why Location Changes Everything

A 0,000 salary in Austin, TX is not the same as 0,000 in San Francisco. State income taxes, housing costs, and everyday expenses can make the same number feel like a completely different salary.

Remote vs in-officeFull remote work lets you live in a lower-cost area while earning a higher-cost-city salary — potentially the biggest compensation arbitrage available today.

Questions to Ask Before You Accept

  • What does the company contribute toward health insurance premiums each month?
  • What is the 401(k) match — and what is the vesting schedule?
  • Is this a high-deductible health plan? Is an HSA offered and does the company contribute to it?
  • What is the bonus structure — is it guaranteed or discretionary?
  • How many PTO days, and are they use-it-or-lose-it?
  • Is there a remote or hybrid option, and could I live in a lower-cost area?
  • What does the career growth path look like — and what's the typical raise at year one?
The bottom line: Never compare two job offers by salary alone. Build a simple spreadsheet with total compensation for each offer — cash, benefits, retirement match, health plan value, and cost-of-living adjustment. The offer that looks smaller on paper is often the better deal.
